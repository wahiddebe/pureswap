How to stake Ethereum (ETH)?

How to stake Ethereum (ETH)?

Written by Pure Swap

3 February 2025

10 min read

Introduction

With Ethereum’s transition to Proof-of-Stake (PoS), staking has become one of the easiest ways to earn passive income while supporting the network. But if you're new to staking, you might be wondering—what is staking, and how do you get started? In this article, we’ll walk you through the basics of Ethereum staking, the benefits and risks, and the different ways you can stake ETH safely.

What is Ethereum Staking?

Ethereum staking involves locking up your ETH to help secure the Ethereum network and validate transactions. In return, you earn rewards—typically in the form of more ETH.

After Ethereum’s Merge in September 2022, the network moved from an energy-intensive Proof-of-Work (PoW) system to Proof-of-Stake (PoS). This means the network is now secured by validators who stake their ETH instead of miners who use computing power.

Why Stake Ethereum?

Here are a few compelling reasons to stake your ETH:

  • Earn Passive Income: Validators earn rewards in ETH for their work.
  • Support the Network: Help decentralize and secure Ethereum.
  • Eco-Friendly: PoS is more sustainable than traditional mining.
  • Better Yield than HODLing: Staking ETH generally offers higher returns than just holding ETH in a wallet.

How to Stake Ethereum (ETH)

  • Eco-Friendly: PoS is more sustainable than traditional mining.
  • Better Yield than HODLing: Staking ETH generally offers higher returns than just holding ETH in a wallet.

1. Solo Staking (Full Validator Node)

  • Requirements: 32 ETH, stable internet connection, technical knowledge.
  • How It Works: You run your own validator node and stake 32 ETH. You’re responsible for maintaining uptime and staying updated.
  • Pros: Full control, maximum rewards.
  • Cons: High barrier to entry, risk of penalties if misconfigured.

2. Staking-as-a-Service

  • Best For: Users who have 32 ETH but don’t want to manage hardware.
  • How It Works: Third-party services like Rocket Pool or StakeWise help you run a validator without handling the technical setup.
  • Pros: Less hassle, secure.
  • Cons: Service fees, you still need 32 ETH.

3. Pooled Staking / Liquid Staking

  • Best For: Users with less than 32 ETH.
  • Popular Platforms: Lido, Rocket Pool, Coinbase, Binance.
  • How It Works: You deposit any amount of ETH and receive a liquid token (e.g., stETH) representing your staked assets.
  • Pros: Accessible to everyone, liquid tokens can be used in DeFi.
  • Cons: Smart contract risk, centralization concerns on some platforms.

4. Centralized Exchange Staking

  • Best For: Beginners.
  • How It Works: Exchanges like Coinbase, Kraken, and Binance allow you to stake ETH directly on their platform.
  • Pros: Simple, user-friendly.
  • Cons: Custodial—your ETH isn’t under your full control.

Things to Consider Before Staking

  • Lock-Up Periods: Some methods lock your ETH for a time.
  • Validator Slashing: Validators can be penalized for bad behavior or downtime.
  • Fees: Some platforms take a cut from your rewards.
  • Decentralization: Staking with large centralized platforms could hurt network decentralization.

Final Thoughts

Staking Ethereum is a powerful way to participate in the future of decentralized finance. Whether you’re a tech-savvy investor or a casual ETH holder, there’s a staking method that fits your needs.

As always, do your own research and consider the risks before staking. Choose platforms and services that prioritize security, transparency, and community.
